# Final Reflection

Campass began as a playful response to a practical problem: new and returning students often experience campus as a set of disconnected buildings, routes, and tasks. Through the combination of a simplified map, NFC check-ins, AR mascot encounters, a message wall, and a collection loop, our design reframes orientation as an active social journey rather than a one-time navigation activity.

The final concept is not only about helping users find places. It is about helping them build memory, confidence, and belonging through repeated interaction with the physical campus.

## 1. Social Implications

The strongest social value of Campass is its potential to turn campus exploration into a shared experience. Features such as the location-based Message Wall and collectible achievements encourage students to leave traces, tips, and stories for others. This can make unfamiliar spaces feel warmer and more approachable, especially for newcomers who may otherwise experience orientation as stressful or isolating.

| Social Value | Design Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Belonging | Shared discoveries and place-based posts help students see campus as a living community rather than a static map. |
| Local Knowledge | Informal tips, stories, and hidden details can make campus knowledge easier to pass between cohorts. |
| Motivation | Gamified progress gives users a reason to revisit spaces and notice details they may otherwise ignore. |

However, the same social layer also introduces risks. A public message wall can become noisy, exclusionary, or harmful if it lacks moderation. Gamification may also unintentionally pressure users to visit every location or compare progress with others. For this reason, Campass should treat social participation as optional, lightweight, and respectful of different comfort levels.

## 2. Ethical Considerations

Because Campass is built around location-based interaction, privacy is the most important ethical concern. NFC check-ins and AR encounters can create meaningful engagement, but they also imply that the system may know where a user has been and what they have unlocked. Even if the prototype stores progress locally, a future production version should minimize data collection and avoid exposing individual movement patterns.

:::info Ethical Design Position
Campass should collect only what is necessary for the experience, clearly explain what is stored, allow users to participate without public posting, and provide moderation tools for any community-generated content.
:::

We also considered accessibility and inclusion. A campus game should not reward only users who can move quickly, visit every spot, or comfortably use AR. Future versions should include alternative ways to unlock content, readable non-AR equivalents, keyboard and screen-reader support, and clear visual contrast. The design should support exploration without making users feel excluded by physical ability, device capability, or social confidence.

## 3. Use of Generative AI

Generative AI was used as a supporting tool during this project, mainly for ideation, writing refinement, code debugging, and documentation structure. It helped us compare design alternatives, improve wording, and accelerate repetitive implementation tasks. It was not treated as a replacement for design judgment, user research, or technical validation.

| AI Use | Boundary |
| --- | --- |
| Brainstorming | AI helped expand possible design directions, but final choices were based on our project goals and prototype constraints. |
| Writing Refinement | AI helped improve clarity and structure, while the team checked whether the claims matched the actual Campass concept. |
| Code Support | AI helped debug and explain implementation problems, but generated code still required manual review and testing. |

The main limitation of using AI is that it can produce confident but generic statements. To avoid this, we grounded final decisions in the actual Campass concept: NFC-based check-ins, AR mascot summoning, the message wall, collection progress, and the performance constraints documented in our implementation. AI helped us move faster, but the responsibility for accuracy, ethics, and design direction remained with the team.

## 4. Final Takeaway

The project shows that a campus guide can be more than a utility tool. When designed carefully, it can become a bridge between physical space, digital play, and social memory. The key challenge is balance: Campass should make exploration more engaging without making it intrusive, competitive, or inaccessible.

Our final reflection is that the most valuable version of Campass is not the most feature-heavy one. It is the version that helps users feel oriented, curious, and connected while still respecting their privacy, attention, and autonomy.
